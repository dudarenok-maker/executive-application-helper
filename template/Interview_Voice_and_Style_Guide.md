## Part 2 — Style adjustments for spoken delivery

*This is where the rhythm, vocabulary and structure change. These adjustments apply broadly across most executive candidates and can stay as-is unless [Candidate First Name] has a specific spoken-delivery convention that differs.*

### 1. Vocabulary — slightly more conversational

Use the same precise, concrete nouns and active verbs as in writing. But where the Writing Voice guide allows specialist terminology selectively, in an interview, specialist terms need a quick gloss the first time they appear — assume the panel may not share the candidate's working vocabulary. *"[Technical term] — [one-line definition]"* on first mention; the unadorned term thereafter.

### 2. Sentence length — shorter than written

In writing, long layered sentences carry complex ideas. In speech, long sentences lose the listener. Aim for ~12–18 words per sentence on average. Break long thoughts into two or three sentences. Follow a long sentence (when used) with a short one that crystallises.

### 3. Pacing — ~140–160 words per minute

A natural executive speaking pace is about 140–160 words per minute. Use this to budget:

- **90-second STAR** = ~210–240 words
- **2-minute STAR** = ~280–320 words
- **3-minute STAR** = ~420–480 words
- **"Tell me about yourself" (2–2.5 min)** = ~280–400 words

In prep documents, draft the spoken text at the target word count. Read aloud; if running long, cut — do not speed up.

### 4. Pause discipline

Pauses are part of the answer. They give the panel time to absorb a result, signal intent, and demonstrate composure. Mark them explicitly in prep documents:

- `*[brief pause]*` — after a thesis sentence; between beats in a long answer; before a result
- `*[pause]*` — at the end of a sub-answer; when handing back to the panel
- `*[stop — pass back to recruiter / chair]*` — at the end of an answer

Resist the urge to fill silence. The panel will follow up if they need to.

### 5. Rhetorical questions — sparingly

In writing, rhetorical questions may be a signature move. In speech, one or two per interview is the limit. They work as openers ("What does it actually take to…?") but used too often in spoken delivery, they feel performative.

### 6. Conversational rhythm — em-dashes become audible pauses

In writing, em-dashes mark a beat. In a prepared answer, they translate into a half-second pause — most often after a key clause. When drafting a STAR for spoken delivery, place em-dashes where the listener should feel the weight.

### 7. First-person, outward-facing — same

Write from "I" but point the lens outward. *"I led the program; the team delivered the outcome."* Personal experience is the entry point, not the destination. Intimacy without self-indulgence.

### 8. Physical and familiar metaphors — even more useful

Concrete, embodied imagery is more memorable aloud than in writing. Preferred sources stay the same as the candidate's written voice — surgery, construction, nature, movement, sport, music, or whatever metaphor sources are documented in the Writing Voice guide. These travel well into an interview room.

### 9. The Three-Beat Structure — preserved, compressed

If the candidate's written voice uses an **assert → complicate → reframe** pattern (or equivalent three-beat structure), it stays for interviews — but compressed inside STAR. In a 90-second STAR:

- **Assert** = Situation + Task in 2–3 sentences (10–15 sec)
- **Complicate** = the genuinely hard part of the Action (15–20 sec)
- **Reframe** = Result + lesson or forward link in 2–3 sentences (10–15 sec)

The middle expands or contracts with the time budget. Assert and Reframe stay tight regardless of length.

## STAR structure — conventions

Every pre-prepared STAR response uses this internal shape:

| Component | What it carries | Target length (90-sec) | Target length (2–3 min) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Situation** | The scene. 1–2 sentences. Name the role, the organisation, the moment. Set the scale (staff, budget, scope) in passing, not as a list. | 25–35 words | 40–60 words |
| **Task** | The challenge. 1 sentence. State what had to be true at the end. | 20–30 words | 30–50 words |
| **Action** | The moves. 3–5 sentences. Name what *the candidate* did — not what "the team" did unless directly relevant. Include 1–2 specific metrics or moves that anchor the answer to evidence. | 100–140 words | 150–220 words |
| **Result** | The outcome + the lesson or forward link. 1–2 sentences. Lead with the measurable outcome. End with what the work taught or how it shapes how the candidate approaches this kind of problem now. | 40–60 words | 60–100 words |

**Do not start an Action paragraph with "We…".** The panel is interviewing the candidate, not the team. Start with "I…" or with the concrete first move ("First, I…").

**Do not bury the Result.** The panel's most attentive moment is the last 15 seconds before they invite the next question. Use it.

## STAR coaching-note conventions

Every pre-prepared STAR response in a prep document closes with coaching notes. The conventions:

- **Heading:** `**Coaching notes.**` (bold paragraph, not a heading)
- **Format:** bullet list, 3–5 bullets, each one short directive sentence
- **Mix:** include 1–2 "do this" reminders + 1–2 "don't do this" traps + 1 time-discipline reminder
- **Tone:** terse, second-person, no qualifiers ("Don't" not "Avoid")

Example pattern:

> **Coaching notes.**
>
> - [Directive — e.g. "Three beats: scale → sector breadth → why-now framing. Don't add a fourth."]
> - [Trap — e.g. "Resist the urge to list every achievement — the recruiter is testing whether you can be concise."]
> - [Conditional drop — e.g. "Drop the [optional detail] if the recruiter is signalling time pressure."]
> - [Anti-pattern — e.g. "Don't open with 'thanks for taking the time' — open with positioning, not pleasantries."]

## Tone by interview format

### Recruiter screen

- **Pace:** Slightly faster than panel. Recruiter is qualifying against a checklist; punchier delivery signals confidence.
- **STAR depth:** 90 seconds. Lead with the strongest one or two anchors per question; abbreviate the rest.
- **Wit and warmth:** A little more permitted. Recruiter is on the candidate's side if they like them.
- **Closing each answer:** Pass back actively. *"Happy to go deeper on any of that."* Recruiters appreciate signposting.
- **What to avoid:** Long preambles, history lessons, evidence stacked beyond the time budget.

### Panel interview (behavioural)

- **Pace:** Measured. The panel is reading composure as much as content.
- **STAR depth:** 2–3 minutes. Earn the depth by anchoring in specific moves, not by adding more examples.
- **Wit and warmth:** Disciplined. One moment per panel, usually in the close.
- **Closing each answer:** End on the result and the lesson. Pause. Let the panel ask the next question.
- **What to avoid:** Multiple examples per question (the panel will ask for a second example if they want one); generic leadership talk without a concrete scene; the "we" trap.

### Executive panel / Secretary-level / Board-level

- **Pace:** Slowest. Strategic register. Less behavioural, more "how would you approach", "what would you do differently", "where would you push back".
- **STAR depth:** 2–3 minutes, but with explicit governance / risk / 30-60-90 framing layered onto STAR. Result paragraph carries forward commitment, not just historical outcome.
- **Wit and warmth:** Minimal. Calm authority is the register.
- **Closing each answer:** Forward-facing. Name what the candidate would carry into the role from the work just described. *"That same discipline is what I would bring to [X] in the first 90 days."*
- **What to avoid:** Defensive answers to gap questions ("I haven't done X"); over-claiming seniority equivalence without naming the formal classification; tactical detail at the expense of strategic register.

## Common interview-question types and how to approach them

### Opening — "Tell me about yourself" / "Walk me through your background"

- **2–2.5 minutes spoken** (~280–400 words).
- **Three beats only:** scale → sector breadth → why-now / why-this-role framing.
- **Drop the chronology trap.** Do not narrate dates and titles. Lead with what the work has been *about*, not where it was *done*.
- **Close on motivation that points forward**, not on a summary of the past.
- The opening sets the panel's expectation of pace and register for the rest of the interview. Treat it as the most important 2 minutes.

### Behavioural questions — "Tell me about a time you…"

- **Full STAR.** Do not skip the Situation.
- **Specific scene, not a category.** *"At [Anchor Organisation], when [specific event]…"* not *"There was an incident management situation…"*.
- **Action paragraph is where the panel learns who the candidate is.** Name the moves, not the meetings.

### Strategic / "How would you approach" questions

- **Honest framing first.** *"I'd start by listening — but the working hypothesis I'd test is X."*
- **30/60/90 framework** is the natural shape. First 30 days = listen; days 30–60 = working hypothesis + early priorities; days 60–90 = visible delivery + operating-rhythm reset.
- **Name the decision the candidate would need from the executive** — relevant CEO, Deputy Secretary, or sponsor. Signals understanding of the governance dynamic.

### Weakness / gap questions

- **Honest acknowledgment, then pivot to closest analogue.** *"I haven't run X. The closest evidence I bring is Y."*
- **Do not over-pivot.** Acknowledge for at least one full sentence before bridging. Cutting the acknowledgment too fast reads as evasive.
- **Name the framing.** *"Adjacent rather than held"* is a phrase that signals self-awareness.
- For lived-experience questions or identity-based gaps, follow the discipline captured in the evidence bank's watch-outs register: do not construct equivalence, lead with closest-analogue service-design or stakeholder-leadership track record + forward commitment.

### Why-this-role / why-now / why-leave-current

- **Three threads:** the work that has landed in the current role; the substance of the new role; the timing.
- **Never speak negatively** about the current organisation, the current leadership or the current operating environment. Frame the close as completion, not departure.
- If pressed on whether the move is the candidate's choice or the organisation's, the honest answer is whichever is true — keep it short, do not over-explain.

### Salary expectations

- **Reflect first, do not quote.** *"Happy to align on [band] expectations. What's the published band for the role?"*
- If pressed, anchor in the **upper half** of the published or known band.
- *"Happy to discuss specifics once we know it's a fit"* is a complete sentence.

### Candidate questions back to the panel

- **Two or three, not five.**
- **Calibrated to signal homework, not flatter.** Best questions reference something specific the panel said earlier in the interview, or name a known organisational dynamic.
- **Avoid:** salary questions at first round; "what's the culture like?" (too generic); anything the candidate pack already answers.

## What stays the same across all interview formats

- **[Spelling / dialect — e.g. Australian English / UK English / US English]** spelling and idiom.
- **No corporate boilerplate.** *"Move the needle"*, *"circle back"*, *"at the end of the day"* — never.
- **Outcomes anchored in numbers** wherever possible. Percentages, team sizes, budgets, timeframes.
- **[Three-beat structure or candidate's equivalent]** preserved, compressed or expanded with the time budget.
- **First-person, outward-facing** perspective. Personal experience is the entry point.
- **Honest acknowledgment of gaps** before pivoting to closest analogue.
- **End answers on forward momentum** or the lesson, not on a summary.

## What changes from written to spoken

- **Sentence length** is shorter than written. Aim for ~12–18 words on average.
- **Pacing** is deliberate. ~140–160 words per minute.
- **Pauses** are explicit and used. Mark them in prep documents.
- **STAR replaces exploratory structure.** The walk-through is not the answer.
- **[Three-beat or candidate's pattern]** is compressed inside STAR's S/T/A/R envelope, not expanded across paragraphs.
